\begin{abstract}
This note presents Thermo Credit as a measurement-first macro-credit
framework. The central question is simple: when new credit goes to different
destinations, how does that change growth, inflation, asset-price pressure,
and fragility? The model has two parts. The first is a stock-flow-consistent
accounting base built from balance-sheet identities. The second is a credit
state layer that tracks capacity, stress, persistence, and adjustment costs.
We use a compact state built around credit partition, capacity, and stress,
including a bookkeeping potential $U(S_{\mathrm{M}},V_{\mathrm{C}},\xi,\ldots)$
with memory variable $\xi$. We also define practical gauges such as
free-energy-like $F_{\mathrm{C}}$ and exergy-like $X_{\mathrm{C}}$. A
Maxwell-like condition is used as a local consistency check, not as a general
law. When it fails, we read the gap as observable non-equilibrium behaviour in
the $(S_{\mathrm{M}},V_{\mathrm{C}})$ plane. The aim is practical: the
quantities should be computable from public data, tested by forecast value,
and released in a form that others can recalculate. In this revision,
region-specific reference parameters are treated as tunable values rather than
fixed constants, and the note states the implicit relation and the calibration
objective more directly.
\end{abstract}

\section{Problem Statement and Scope}

Classical money-first views such as the Quantity Theory of Money (QTM) explain
nominal dynamics through the money stock, its velocity, and policy or shock
terms \cite{Fisher1911,Friedman1956,Laidler1985}. Credit-first views, notably
the Quantity Theory of Credit (QTC), stress that banks create deposits when
they lend and that the use of credit matters for macro-financial outcomes
\cite{Werner2012,BoE2014}. In parallel, information theory and statistical
mechanics have inspired useful analogies for allocation and dispersion
\cite{Theil1967,Foley1994,DragulescuYakovenko2000,Shannon1948,Jaynes1957}.

This note takes a limited and practical step. We build a thermodynamic-style
state description that separates scale from dispersion, adds an explicit
capacity variable for bank balance sheets, and yields testable constraints and
early-warning gauges. The claim is not that macro data follow physical laws.
The claim is narrower: a disciplined bookkeeping analogy can help supervision,
stress testing\K{18}, and systematic monitoring.

\subsection*{Research target and use cases}

The measurement-first revision narrows the framework to one question: how does
the destination of new credit change the path from credit expansion to growth,
inflation, asset-price acceleration, and fragility? In practical terms, the
current version serves three use cases:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item early-warning signals for asset inflation and systemic fragility;
  \item description of the GDP-linked versus existing-asset credit mix rather
        than credit volume alone;
  \item forecast improvement relative to total-credit-only baselines.
\end{enumerate}
This keeps the theory tied to a concrete mapping from inputs to outputs. It
also stops the thermodynamic language from turning into a loose metaphor.

\subsection*{Related Work}

Several research strands motivate this mapping. Market microstructure work has
already proposed temperature- and entropy-like summaries of liquidity, but it
mainly stays at the order-book level and does not address credit allocation or
regulatory headroom \cite{Li2024}. Work on model risk also uses
thermodynamic-style language, but not in a bank balance-sheet setting
\cite{Feng2019}.

This note works at a coarser scale. It builds $(S_M,V_C,T_L,p_C)$ from
balance-sheet aggregates, credit allocation shares, and market-stress proxies.
The aim is not to reproduce thermodynamics in full. It is to create a compact
macro-credit state description that can be tested against growth, spread, and
fragility outcomes.

\section{Credit Partition and State Variables}

We work over a chosen system (jurisdiction, sector set) and time aggregation
(e.g.\ monthly). Let $\Min$ denote money-in-circulation\K{10} over this domain,
and let $\{q_i\}$ be a stable, MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively
Exhaustive)\K{15} partition of its uses (real activity, housing credit,
securities margin, etc.), with
\[
  \sum_i q_i = 1.
\]

\subsection{Measurement-first overlay: credit partition and targets}

To make the framework operational, we add a minimal measurement-first overlay.
Its purpose is narrower than the full thermodynamic analogy. The working
question is: how does the destination of new credit change the translation of
credit expansion into output, prices, asset prices, and fragility? In that
sense, the overlay is close to Werner's destination-of-credit view: the main
issue is not credit in the abstract, but whether new credit funds GDP-linked
transactions or mainly bids up existing assets.

The smallest useful state vector is
\[
  \left( C_t, q_t, Y_t^N, Y_t^R, P_t, A_t, S_t \right),
\]
where $C_t$ denotes new credit flow during period $t$, $q_t \in [0,1]$ is the
share of that flow directed to GDP-linked transactions, $Y_t^N$ is a nominal
output proxy used for accounting ratios, $Y_t^R$ is a real-activity target,
$P_t$ is a broad price-level proxy, $A_t$ is an asset-price or balance-sheet
accumulation proxy for existing assets, and $S_t$ is a stress / fragility
state. The coarse GDP-linked-versus-existing-asset split is then
\begin{equation}
  C_t^R = q_t C_t,
  \qquad
  C_t^A = (1-q_t) C_t.
  \label{eq:credit_partition}
\end{equation}

This partition share $q_t$ should not be confused with the entropy shares
$\{q_i\}$ used below. The scalar $q_t$ classifies \emph{new credit flows}
into GDP-linked and existing-asset destinations. By contrast, $\{q_i\}$
partitions broader money and credit uses for the entropy construction. The two
objects are related, but they are not the same.

The labels should also be read with care. In the present note, $C_t^R$ is
short for credit that is closely linked to current output, income, or new
production. $C_t^A$ is short for credit that mainly finances purchases of
existing land, existing housing, or financial assets. Construction credit sits
between these two cases: it can add to current GDP, but it can also feed
collateral and land-price cycles. In applied work it may therefore need a
partial weight rather than a hard bucket.

Identification of $q_t$ is best handled in layers: (i) direct observation from
sectoral lending and loan-purpose data when available, (ii) proxy inference
from housing, land, equity, investment, and stress variables when direct
splits are incomplete, and (iii) latent-state estimation only after the
reduced-form version is stable.

\subsection{Monetary dispersion entropy}

We define an entropy-like extensive index
\begin{equation}
  \SM = k \Min H(q), 
  \qquad
  H(q) \equiv - \sum_i q_i \log q_i,
  \label{eq:SM_def}
\end{equation}
where $k>0$ is a conventional scaling constant. This mirrors ideal mixing
entropy $\Delta S_{\mathrm{mix}} \propto N H(x)$ and follows the
information-theoretic form of Shannon \cite{Shannon1948}. $S_{\mathrm{M}}$
grows with both scale and dispersion; concentration (reallocation into fewer
uses) reduces $H(q)$ and can reduce $S_{\mathrm{M}}$ even at fixed $\Min$.

Formally, $H(q)$ is the Shannon--Boltzmann entropy of the MECE
bucket probabilities $\{q_i\}$ with natural logarithm (no Tsallis, R\'enyi,
or alternative families are used here).
In addition to the extensive $S_{\mathrm{M}}$, the implementation
tracks a scale-free version
$S_{\mathrm{M,hat}} = H(q)/\log K$ (with $K$ buckets) and per-bucket
contributions $S_{\mathrm{M,in}}^{(i)} = k M_{\mathrm{in}} \tilde q_i$.
For the baseline JP configuration these buckets correspond to
``productive'', ``housing'', ``consumption'', ``financial'', and
``government'' allocations (recorded as
${q_{\text{productive}},q_{\text{housing}},q_{\text{consumption}},
q_{\text{financial}},q_{\text{government}}}$); regional variants use
analogous coarse splits in their allocation tables, always normalised so
that $\sum_i q_i = 1$.

\paragraph{Energy and entropy conventions.}
The internal potential $U(S_{\mathrm{M}},V_{\mathrm{C}},\ldots)$ is a bookkeeping scalar in currency units, 
and the derived quantities $F_{\mathrm{C}}$, $X_{\mathrm{C}}$, and $G_{\mathrm{C}}$ are free-energy- and 
exergy-like gauges constructed from $(U,S_{\mathrm{M}},V_{\mathrm{C}})$ and fixed environmental parameters $(T_0,p_0)$. 
They are interpreted as indicators of usable credit capacity and policy room, not as literal physical energies; 
general caveats on the analogy are collected in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Capacity and conjugate variables}

We introduce:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\VC$: an effective credit capacity or headroom\K{4} (e.g.\ CET1/RWA\K{3}
        slack, HQLA (High Quality Liquid Assets)\K{6}-based lending space,
        liquidity buffers\K{7});
  \item $\TL$: a liquidity-intensity index (a ``temperature-like'' proxy from
        spreads, turnover, depth);
  \item $\pC$: a ``credit pressure''\K{5} (shadow price of capacity).
\end{itemize}

We use a quasi-potential (bookkeeping energy) with an explicit memory variable
$\xi$ that captures slow-moving structural distortions (regime, collateral and
haircut conventions, concentration, funding fragility, etc.):
\begin{equation}
  U = U(\SM, \VC, \xi, \ldots).
  \label{eq:U_def}
\end{equation}
We then define reference conjugate quantities
\begin{equation}
  \TL \equiv \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial \SM} \right)_{\VC,\xi},
  \qquad
  \pC \equiv - \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial \VC} \right)_{\SM,\xi}.
  \label{eq:conjugate}
\end{equation}
Here $U$ is not a physical internal energy; it is a bookkeeping potential
chosen so that a first-law-like decomposition holds. Classical QTM/QTC do
not name $U$; we introduce it as an analytic device.
At the level of the abstract mapping, \eqref{eq:conjugate} should be read
as a reference structure: in an idealized state description there exists
some smooth potential $U_{\mathrm{th}}(S_M,V_C)$ whose canonical fields
$(T_L^{\mathrm{th}},p_C^{\mathrm{th}})$ are given by these derivatives.
The empirical objects $U(t)$, $T_L(t)$, and $p_C(t)$ used in this note
are proxy series constructed from GDP-like aggregates, liquidity metrics,
and regulatory headroom; they are chosen to be interpretable and to track
the intended directions, but they are not literally defined by
finite-difference approximations to \eqref{eq:conjugate} in the code.

\paragraph{Non-equilibrium entropy balance.}
In an open macro-financial system, a monotonic ``second law'' for $\SM$ is not
appropriate. Instead we work with an entropy-like balance
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}\SM}{\mathrm{d}t} = \Phi_S(t) + \sigma_S(t),
  \qquad \sigma_S(t) \ge 0,
  \label{eq:SM_balance}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi_S$ is an entropy flow induced by external coupling (policy, foreign
flows, institutional shifts) and $\sigma_S$ is a non-negative entropy production
rate associated with irreversible credit reallocation and market fragmentation.
In this note we treat $\sigma_S$ operationally and proxy it using hysteresis and
loop-area diagnostics (Section~\ref{sec:insights}); $\SM$ itself may rise or fall
depending on the sign of $\Phi_S$.

Empirically, $U(t)$ is instantiated as a scalar credit-capacity gauge constructed 
from GDP-like or credit aggregates. When a dedicated $U$ series is not available, 
the implementation fills it using a conservative fallback order over available GDP-like 
and credit-stock series (see Appendix, Internal-energy gauge). 
An optional detrended version $U^\star$ is used only for diagnostics and visualisation 
(to study fluctuations around a slowly varying baseline), 
while the core potentials $\FC$ and $\XC$ are computed from the undetrended $U$.

\subsection{Operational metrics and irreversibility}

The v2 overlay turns the thermodynamic language into directly computable
operators. A minimal set of empirical ratios is
\begin{align}
  \eta_t &\equiv \frac{\Delta Y_t^N}{C_t},
  \label{eq:eta_def} \\
  d_t &\equiv \frac{\Delta A_t}{C_t},
  \label{eq:dt_def} \\
  \sigma_t &\equiv \max\{0, 1-\eta_t\},
  \label{eq:sigma_proxy_def}
\end{align}
where $\eta_t$ is a credit-efficiency proxy, $d_t$ is an asset-bias proxy, and
$\sigma_t$ is a deliberately simple dissipation proxy. When only an asset-price
index is available, a normalized alternative is
\begin{equation}
  d_t^{\mathrm{proxy}}
  \equiv
  \frac{\Delta \log A_t}{C_t / Y_t^N}.
  \label{eq:dt_proxy_def}
\end{equation}

To make the ``Thermo'' label empirically useful, we also need a minimal
irreversibility block. The reduced-form version used in the current v2 draft is
\begin{align}
  \eta_t
    &= \eta_0 - \alpha(1-q_t) - \beta S_{t-1} + \varepsilon_{\eta,t},
    \label{eq:eta_dyn} \\
  S_t
    &= \rho S_{t-1}
      + \gamma \frac{C_t^A}{Y_t^N}
      - \delta \frac{C_t^R}{Y_t^N}
      + \varepsilon_{S,t},
    \label{eq:stress_dyn}
\end{align}
so that credit flowing into existing-asset transactions raises fragility while
accumulated stress lowers next-period conversion efficiency.

These expressions are not presented as a full structural law. They are a
measurement-first bridge from theory to data. Existing diagnostics such as
$\SM$, $\TL$, $\pC$, loop area, $U$, $\FC$, and $\XC$ should therefore be read
as companion indicators and possible inputs into the stress block $S_t$, not
as substitutes for the partition variables $(C_t^R, C_t^A, q_t)$.

\subsection{Depth and turnover proxies}

To construct the liquidity temperature $\TL$ and related gauges from observable
series, we introduce two derived inputs: a \emph{depth} proxy $D$ and a
\emph{turnover} proxy $\Theta$. They are practical inputs rather than state
variables in their own right. Depth is usually a rescaled version of
$L_{\mathrm{real}}$, while turnover is usually a safe version of
$U/L_{\mathrm{real}}$. The implementation clips or fills pathological cases so
that one noisy observation does not dominate the temperature measure.

\paragraph{Design of the liquidity temperature.}
The liquidity temperature is designed to be high when spreads are narrow,
markets are deep, and turnover is brisk. It should fall sharply when any one
of these ingredients becomes stressed.
To this end we combine standardized components
$(z_s,z_d,z_t)$---the z-scores of inverse spreads, inverse depth, and
turnover---into a composite
\[
  \hat T_{\mathrm{mult}}(t) = z_s(t)\,z_d(t)\,\bigl(1 + \tfrac{1}{2} z_t(t)\bigr),
\]
and then rescale to $T_{\mathrm{L}} \in [0,1]$ by min--max normalisation.
We use this multiplicative form as the baseline because it encodes a simple
``weakest link'' logic: severe stress in one component should pull the whole
temperature measure down.

\subsection{Units and scaling conventions}

The units are consistent but conventional. $\Min$, $L_{\mathrm{real}}$, $U$,
and $\VC$ are monetary or currency-like aggregates; $\TL$ and $\pC$ are
dimensionless indices; and $S_M = k\Min H(q)$ scales with money volume and
dispersion. In applications, signs, changes, and relative levels matter more
than absolute magnitudes. That is why the empirical section reports a tuned
implicit headroom score rather than treating raw nominal $X_C$ levels as
directly comparable across regions.

\subsection{SFC conservation backbone (accounting first law)}
\label{sec:sfc_backbone}

For early-warning use, we anchor the framework in stock--flow consistent (SFC)
accounting. This is the non-negotiable conservation layer. It is not an
analogy, but a set of identities implied by double-entry bookkeeping and
flow-of-funds accounts.

\paragraph{Sectoral financial balances.}
Let $B_s(t)$ denote the net lending/borrowing (financial balance) of sector $s$
over a period $t$ (positive for net lenders, negative for net borrowers).
Using a minimal, data-friendly three-sector aggregation
\(s\in\{P,G,R\}\) (domestic private, government, rest of world), SFC implies
\begin{equation}
  B_P(t) + B_G(t) + B_R(t) = 0.
  \label{eq:sfc_balance}
\end{equation}
In practice $B_G$ is available as the general-government net lending/borrowing
(balance), and $B_R$ is closely related to the current account (up to sign and
conventions). When a direct private-sector net lending series is unavailable,
$B_P$ can be computed residually from \eqref{eq:sfc_balance}, which is often the
most robust route in public-data pipelines.

\paragraph{Stock--flow identity.}
Let $A_s(t)$ be a stock variable associated with sector $s$ (e.g., net financial
assets or a balance-sheet aggregate available at the chosen frequency). The SFC
update is
\begin{equation}
  A_s(t) = A_s(t-1) + B_s(t) + \mathrm{VAL}_s(t),
  \label{eq:sfc_stockflow}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{VAL}_s(t)$ collects valuation/reclassification effects. For
monitoring, one can either (i) work with series where valuation is small at the
aggregation level, (ii) treat $\mathrm{VAL}_s$ as an explicit residual, or (iii)
use detrended / change-based diagnostics that are less sensitive to level
revisions.

\paragraph{Interpretation inside Thermo-Credit.}
Equations \eqref{eq:sfc_balance}--\eqref{eq:sfc_stockflow} provide the hard
constraint layer. The ``energy-like'' decomposition that follows is a
structured way to attribute observed movements to dispersion ($\SM$), capacity
($\VC$), policy work, and non-equilibrium losses, while still respecting the
SFC backbone.

When a bank grants a new loan, it simultaneously creates a matching deposit.
On the joint bank--customer system, this is a pure accounting operation
(``loans create deposits''), consistent with the SFC backbone in
Section~\ref{sec:sfc_backbone}. Within our mapping, the associated change in the
potential $U$ is decomposed as
\begin{equation}
  \Delta U \approx \bar{T}_{\!L} \,\Delta \SM
               - \bar{p}_{\mathrm{C}} \,\Delta \VC
               + \bar{A}\,\Delta \xi
               + W_{\mathrm{policy}} - \mathcal{D} + \varepsilon,
  \label{eq:first_law}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\Delta \SM$: change in dispersion entropy from scale and allocation
        shifts (typically $\Delta \SM > 0$ in net expansion);
  \item $\Delta \VC$: change in effective capacity
        ($\Delta \VC < 0$ when headroom is used up; then
        $-\bar{p}_{\mathrm{C}} \Delta \VC > 0$);
  \item $\Delta \xi$: change in a memory / distortion variable capturing slow
        structural shifts (regime, collateral conventions, concentration,
        funding fragility). $\bar A$ is its conjugate ``affinity''.
  \item $\mathcal{D} \ge 0$: a dissipation term capturing irreversible loss of
        usable credit capacity due to hysteresis, fragmentation, and forced
        balance-sheet adjustments.
  \item $W_{\mathrm{policy}}$: structured policy work\K{14} from regulation,
        guarantees, central bank operations, and other deliberate
        interventions;
  \item $\varepsilon$: residual term capturing model error and omitted
        channels.
\end{itemize}

Credit creation phases (new lending $>$ repayments) and contraction phases
(repayments $>$ new lending) then exhibit characteristic sign patterns for
$(\Delta \Min, \Delta \SM, \Delta \VC, -\bar{p}_{\mathrm{C}} \Delta \VC)$,
which can be tabulated and compared with data. The value of
\eqref{eq:first_law} is diagnostic. It forces us to attribute changes to
dispersion, capacity use, or policy work\K{14} instead of conflating them.

For intuition it is useful to summarize typical sign patterns.
Table~\ref{tab:sign_patterns} collects a minimal classification in terms
of net expansion/contraction and whether headroom is being used or

In addition to these pointwise diagnostics, we can study the \emph{loop
area} traced out by trajectories in the $(\pC,\VC)$ plane over policy or
credit cycles.
In an idealized reversible limit (no dissipation, fixed $\xi$), the line integral
would vanish for closed cycles $\mathcal{C}$, reflecting path-independence on the
local equilibrium manifold.
In practice, non-zero loop area measures
irreversibility and dissipative effects analogous to hysteresis in magnetic
systems or frictional cycles. The streaming estimator in the code updates
an exponentially weighted version of this integral via
\[
  W_t = \lambda W_{t-1}
        + \pC(t-1)\bigl(\VC(t)-\VC(t-1)\bigr),
\]
with forgetting factor $\lambda$ chosen so that old cycles gradually lose
influence.

Large or persistent values of $W_t$ therefore flag regimes where policy
paths in $(\pC,\VC)$ space exhibit pronounced hysteresis and stress.

\subsection{Dissipation and a credit quality factor $Q$}

In the data, irreversibility appears as non-zero loop area in the
$(p_{\mathrm{C}},V_{\mathrm{C}})$ plane. In physics, a similar idea is
captured by the quality factor $Q$ of an oscillator: it compares stored
energy with the energy lost in one cycle. Here we define an analogous
quality factor for the credit system.

Let $\mathrm{loop\_area}(t)$ be the dissipative part of the policy or
credit cycle in period $t$ and let $X_{\mathrm{C}}(t)$ be the
exergy-like ``usable credit capacity'' at time $t$.
We first define a simple loss ratio
\begin{equation}
  \phi(t) = \frac{\mathrm{loop\_area}(t)}{X_{\mathrm{C}}(t-1)},
\end{equation}
whenever $X_{\mathrm{C}}(t-1) > 0$.
When $X_{\mathrm{C}}(t-1) \le 0$, $\phi(t)$ and $Q_{\mathrm{C}}(t)$ are left undefined (treated as missing) in the baseline implementation.
This tells us what fraction of available exergy is lost through policy
hysteresis in that period.
We then define the credit quality factor
\begin{equation}
  Q_{\mathrm{C}}(t)
    = \frac{2\pi}{\phi(t)}
    = 2\pi \, \frac{X_{\mathrm{C}}(t-1)}{\mathrm{loop\_area}(t)}.
\end{equation}

A large $Q_{\mathrm{C}}$ means that the system loses only a small share of
usable credit capacity in each cycle. A small $Q_{\mathrm{C}}$ means that even
modest policy loops destroy a meaningful part of $X_{\mathrm{C}}$, which is
consistent with a fragile or strongly path-dependent credit structure.

For mechanical oscillators, a standard first-order approximation is
\(
  \Delta E / E \approx -2\pi/Q.
\)
By analogy, we can write
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\Delta X_{\mathrm{C}}(t)}{X_{\mathrm{C}}(t-1)}
    \approx - \frac{2\pi}{Q_{\mathrm{C}}(t)}
    + \mathrm{external\_injection}(t) + \varepsilon_t,
\end{equation}
where the external-injection term collects net additions of usable
credit capacity (for example, through large-scale asset purchases or
recapitalisation episodes).
In this way $Q_{\mathrm{C}}$ acts as a simple, dimensionless summary of
how much hysteresis the credit system can tolerate before its usable
capacity is eroded.

\paragraph{Interpreting $W_{\mathrm{policy}}$.}
In the abstract decomposition \eqref{eq:first_law}, $W_{\mathrm{policy}}$
collects structured interventions (policy-rate shifts, large-scale asset
purchases, regulatory changes) that are not captured by the dispersion and
capacity-use terms.
In the reference implementation we do not estimate
$W_{\mathrm{policy}}(t)$ separately inside the indicator pipeline. Instead we
compute the finite-difference terms
$\Delta U$, $\bar T_{\!L}\Delta \SM$, and $-\bar p_{\mathrm{C}}\Delta \VC$
and treat the residual $\Delta U - (\bar T_{\!L}\Delta \SM - \bar
p_{\mathrm{C}}\Delta \VC)$ (the ``first-law residual'') as a combined bucket
for policy work and model error.
For case studies or event analyses, users can overlay simple parametric
forms---for example, specifying
$W_{\mathrm{policy}}(t) = \gamma \mathbf{1}\{t \in [t_0,t_1]\}$ around a
major programme such as a large-scale asset purchase episode, or using
regression-style dummies---to attribute part of the residual to known
policy actions while leaving the remainder as $\varepsilon$.

\subsection{Free Energy, Exergy, and Early-Warning Gauges}

We seek a scalar gauge that (i) decreases as dispersion rises under a fixed
environment, and (ii) upper-bounds structured work extractable over a cycle.
A Helmholtz-style free energy provides this.

For a fixed reference environment $(T_0)$, define
\begin{equation}
  \FC \equiv U - T_0 \SM.
  \label{eq:FC_def}
\end{equation}
Then, under suitable regularity conditions,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{d} \FC = - \pC \,\mathrm{d} \VC + \delta W_{\mathrm{other}},
  \label{eq:dFC}
\end{equation}
so that $\FC$ plays the role of an available-potential measure. As
$\Delta \FC \to 0$ under chosen boundaries, policy headroom for structured
adjustments is effectively exhausted.

When an ambient pressure-like parameter $p_0$ is also relevant, an
exergy-like functional is
\begin{equation}
  \XC = (U - U_0) + p_0 ( \VC - V_{\mathrm{C},0} )
        - T_0 ( \SM - S_{\mathrm{M},0} ),
  \label{eq:XC_def}
\end{equation}
which reduces to $-\Delta \FC$ if $\VC$ is fixed and $p_0$ effects are
negligible. Because $\XC$ depends on boundary choices $(T_0, p_0)$, we treat
it as an optional, environment-dependent gauge.

In principle $\XC$ can take either sign, depending on the chosen
reference state $(U_0,V_{\mathrm{C},0},S_{\mathrm{M},0})$ and
environmental parameters $(T_0,p_0)$.
Negative values indicate that the current configuration has lower
``available credit energy'' than the reference; for early-warning
visualisation, however, it is often convenient to enforce a
non-negative floor (by clipping or by subtracting the minimum value over
the sample) so that zero marks the effective boundary of usable
headroom.

For regime diagnostics it is also useful to separate surplus and
shortage components relative to a fixed free-energy reference.
Let $\Delta F_C(t) = F_C(t) - F_C^{\mathrm{ref}}$ for some chosen
baseline $F_C^{\mathrm{ref}}$ (e.g.\ the first non-missing observation
or a user-specified date).
We then define
\[
  X_C^+(t) = \max\{0,\;\Delta F_C(t)\},
  \qquad
  X_C^-(t) = \max\{0,\;-\Delta F_C(t)\},
\]
so that $X_C^+$ tracks ``excess'' free energy above the reference and
$X_C^-$ tracks shortfall.
These series enter plots and summary tables as complementary early-warning
gauges: persistent build-up of $X_C^+$ suggests unusually large policy
room relative to the reference regime, whereas elevated $X_C^-$ points
to sustained pressure towards the boundary of feasible adjustment.

A Gibbs-style free energy
\begin{equation}
  \GC \equiv U + p_0 \VC - T_0 \SM
  \label{eq:GC_def}
\end{equation}
can be used when $(T_0, p_0)$ are the natural controls. In applications, we
typically monitor $\FC$ (fixed environment) and compare with $\XC$ or $\GC$
when capacity constraints are explicit (cf.\ exergy notions in
\cite{Wall1977}).


\paragraph{External coupling.}
In applications we also allow the credit system to couple to an external
``pressure bath'' and ``temperature bath''. The coupling is built in two
stages. First, we map a small set of market driver series into standardised
monthly processes.
For JP, the pressure drivers are conceptually:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the US high-yield option-adjusted spread (HY OAS), used in level
        form as a proxy for global credit stress;
  \item the US--JP 10Y yield spread,
        $\Delta y_{US,JP}(t) = y_{US}^{10\mathrm{Y}}(t) - y_{JP}^{10\mathrm{Y}}(t)$;
  \item USD/JPY log returns,
        $r_{\mathrm{FX}}(t) = \log \mathrm{USDJPY}(t)
        - \log \mathrm{USDJPY}(t-\Delta t)$.
\end{itemize}
The temperature driver is the VIX equity volatility index, again mapped
to monthly frequency.
Each raw series $X_k(t)$ is aggregated to month-start, transformed according
to its role (level, spread, log-return), and converted to a z-score
\[
  Z_k(t) = \frac{X_k(t) - \mathbb{E}[X_k]}{\sigma[X_k]},
\]
using sample mean and standard deviation over the available history.
Second, we form composite indices by simple averaging over the available
standardised drivers:
\[
  E_p(t) = \frac{1}{K_p} \sum_{k \in \mathcal{P}} Z_k(t),
  \qquad
  E_T(t) = \frac{1}{K_T} \sum_{k \in \mathcal{T}} Z_k(t),
\]
where $\mathcal{P}$ indexes pressure drivers and $\mathcal{T}$ indexes
temperature drivers.
Missing values in individual $Z_k$ are ignored on each date so long as at
least one driver remains.
Equal weights reflect an agnostic prior about the relative importance of each
driver. With only a handful of series, more elaborate factor models or
time-varying loadings would be weakly identified and would risk overfitting.
The unweighted mean therefore serves as a transparent first pass. In future
work one could replace this averaging with a statistically estimated factor
structure or region-specific loadings once longer histories and stronger
identification are available.

Given $E_p$ and $E_T$ we work with effective fields
\[
  p_{\mathrm{C}}^{\mathrm{eff}}(t)
    = p_{\mathrm{C}}(t) + \alpha E_p(t),
  \qquad
  T_{\mathrm{L}}^{\mathrm{eff}}(t)
    = T_{\mathrm{L}}(t) + \delta E_T(t),
\]
so that external stress and risk-on/off conditions act as linear
perturbations to the internal equation of state.
When $\alpha$ or $\delta$ are set to zero the system is ``isolated'' in
this sense; non-zero couplings encode how exposed the credit state is to
external financial conditions.

\paragraph{Sectoral ``chemical potentials''.}
Given the MECE allocation shares $\{q_i\}$ and $M_{\mathrm{in}}$, we can
form sectoral analogues of chemical potentials.
Using the normalised shares $\tilde q_i$ from the entropy construction,
one defines
\[
  \mu_i
    = T_0 k M_{\mathrm{in}} \bigl( \log \tilde q_i + 1 \bigr),
\]
so that, up to constants, $\mu_i$ is the derivative of the entropy-like
term with respect to the bucket mass.
For interpretation we work with deviations from the cross-bucket mean,
\(
  \Delta\mu_i = \mu_i - \bar\mu
\),
where $\bar\mu$ is the average over $i$ at each date.
Large positive $\Delta\mu_i$ flag categories where incremental ``credit
mass'' is unusually valuable in entropy terms; large negative values flag
categories where allocations are already heavy relative to the rest of
the system.
In the current implementation these $\mu_i$ and $\Delta\mu_i$ are used as
diagnostic series and are not themselves fed back into the flow dynamics.


\subsection{Maxwell-Like Relation, Exterior Derivative, and Legendre Structure}

In the local-equilibrium limit where $U(\SM,\VC,\xi)$ is approximately state-like
with $\xi$ effectively fixed over the sampling window, mixed partial derivatives
commute and we obtain a Maxwell-like condition:
\begin{equation}
  \left( \frac{\partial \TL}{\partial \VC} \right)_{\SM,\xi}
  = - \left( \frac{\partial \pC}{\partial \SM} \right)_{\VC,\xi}.
  \label{eq:Maxwell}
\end{equation}
Outside this limit, the failure of integrability is informative rather than a
fatal inconsistency.

Geometrically, the pair $(\TL,\pC)$ defines a differential one-form on the
observed $(\SM,\VC)$ state plane,
\begin{equation}
  \omega \equiv \TL\,\mathrm{d}\SM - \pC\,\mathrm{d}\VC.
  \label{eq:oneform}
\end{equation}
In the integrable (state-like) regime this one-form is (approximately) exact and
can be written as $\omega\approx\mathrm{d}U_{\text{rev}}$ on a local-equilibrium
manifold where $\xi$ is effectively constant over the window. Outside this
regime, $\omega$ should be treated as a convenient field representation rather
than as the differential of a globally defined potential.

Exactness is equivalent to vanishing exterior derivative $\mathrm{d}\omega=0$
(no ``curl''), which in coordinates yields the Maxwell-like condition
\eqref{eq:Maxwell}. To quantify departures from integrability, we introduce a
non-equilibrium ``vorticity'' (curl) measure
\begin{equation}
  \Omega(\SM,\VC)
  \equiv \left( \frac{\partial \TL}{\partial \VC} \right)_{\SM}
        + \left( \frac{\partial \pC}{\partial \SM} \right)_{\VC}.
  \label{eq:vorticity}
\end{equation}
Thus $\Omega\approx 0$ corresponds to the integrable limit (local state-like
behaviour with fixed $\xi$), whereas persistent large $|\Omega|$ indicates strong
path dependence / structural distortions and motivates modelling $\xi$
explicitly rather than forcing a single-potential representation.

This also clarifies the Legendre structure. Starting from $U(\SM,\VC)$ we can,
in principle, define a potential written in terms of the intensive variables,
\[
  \Phi(\TL,\pC)
    \equiv U(\SM,\VC) - \TL \SM + \pC \VC,
\]
where $(\SM,\VC)$ are now regarded as functions of $(\TL,\pC)$ via the
equation of state. Taking the differential and using
\eqref{eq:conjugate} one finds
\[
  \mathrm{d}\Phi = - \SM\,\mathrm{d}\TL + \VC\,\mathrm{d}\pC,
\]
so that
\(
  (\partial \Phi / \partial \TL)_{\pC} = -\SM
\)
and
\(
  (\partial \Phi / \partial \pC)_{\TL} = \VC
\).
This Legendre transform from $(\SM,\VC)$ to $(\TL,\pC)$ is locally well-defined
only when \eqref{eq:Maxwell} holds on an approximately integrable manifold.
The free-energy-type potentials introduced in \S5, such as $\FC$ and $\GC$,
can then be viewed as partially Legendre-transformed versions of $U$
evaluated at fixed environmental parameters $(T_0,p_0)$.

Empirically, one can estimate $\TL(\SM,\VC)$ and $\pC(\SM,\VC)$ from proxy
series and test whether \eqref{eq:Maxwell} approximately holds. Because $U$,
$T_L$, and $p_C$ are built from partly independent proxies rather than from a
single fitted potential, the relation is not enforced by construction.
Instead, it works as an integrability diagnostic. Persistent, large violations
suggest non-equilibrium behaviour, changing $\xi$, or proxy misspecification.
In this sense, the analogy is falsifiable rather than purely rhetorical, and
Section~\ref{sec:insights} summarizes a simple set of operational diagnostics
built from this condition.

\section{Practical Tests and Falsifiability}\label{sec:insights}

The mapping yields several operational diagnostics:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Integrability / vorticity test.} Estimate local derivatives in
        \eqref{eq:Maxwell} and compute the vorticity $\Omega$ in
        \eqref{eq:vorticity}. Large, persistent $|\Omega|$ is interpreted as a
        non-equilibrium signal (path dependence / structural distortions), not
        merely ``model failure''.

  \item \textbf{Work vs.\ dispersion decomposition.} Use \eqref{eq:first_law}
        to decompose changes in $U$ into heat-like dispersion
        $(\bar{T}_{\!L}\Delta \SM)$, capacity use
        $(-\bar{p}_{\mathrm{C}} \Delta \VC)$, and policy work
        $(W_{\mathrm{policy}})$.

  \item \textbf{Free-energy and exergy ceilings.} Monitor $\FC$ or $\XC$ as
        early-warning gauges: sustained proximity to zero under stress
        scenarios flags limited room for non-disruptive adjustment.

  \item \textbf{Loop area and hysteresis.} Compute loop areas in $(\pC,\VC)$
        over policy cycles. Non-zero areas capture dissipative stress and
        irreversibility in credit allocation dynamics\K{17}.

  \item \textbf{Entropy production proxy.} Use loop area, $\mathcal{D}$, and
        changes in $Q_{\mathrm{C}}$ as operational proxies for the entropy
        production rate $\sigma_S$ in \eqref{eq:SM_balance}. Rising dissipation
        and falling $Q_{\mathrm{C}}$ serve as early-warning signals of
        irreversible stress build-up.
\end{enumerate}

All of these rely on observable or reconstructible quantities from public
balance-sheet and market data. Implementation details such as proxy choices,
normalisation, and robustness checks remain part of ongoing empirical work and
should be reported alongside any results.

\subsection{Testable hypotheses}

The reduced-form version of Thermo Credit should be judged by a short list of
predictive hypotheses rather than by conceptual elegance alone:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{H1.} Lower $q_t$ should predict stronger asset-price
        acceleration before it predicts stronger real growth.
  \item \textbf{H2.} Higher $C_t$ with lower $q_t$ should show weaker
        pass-through to inflation and GDP-linked activity than equally large
        credit expansions with higher $q_t$.
  \item \textbf{H3.} High $C_t^A$ combined with high $S_t$ should predict
        greater future volatility, spread widening, or contraction risk.
\end{itemize}

This yields explicit rejection criteria. In the measurement-first reading of
the model, the framework is weakened if:
\begin{itemize}
  \item separating $C_t^R$ and $C_t^A$ does not improve forecasting or
        regime classification relative to total-credit-only baselines;
  \item estimated declines in $q_t$ fail to line up with later asset
        acceleration or fragility build-up;
  \item adding the stress state $S_t$ does not improve downside prediction
        beyond the existing thermo-monitoring indicators.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implicit state relation and iterative calibration}

At this point the practical weakness is clear. If $(T_0,p_0,U_0,V_0,S_0)$ are
left as fixed convenience constants, then $X_C$ is only a transformation of
existing series and can easily lose economic meaning. The operational version
therefore treats the state as \emph{implicitly} defined by observables and a
region-specific parameter vector
\[
  \theta_r \equiv (T_{0,r}, p_{0,r}, U_{0,r}, V_{0,r}, S_{0,r}).
\]
The minimum implicit system is
\begin{align}
  0 &= g_{1,t}(\theta_r)
     \equiv X_C^{\theta_r}(t)
       - \Bigl[(U_t-U_{0,r}) + p_{0,r}(V_{C,t}-V_{0,r})
       - T_{0,r}(S_{M,t}-S_{0,r})\Bigr],
     \label{eq:implicit_xc} \\
  0 &= g_{2,t}(\theta_r)
     \equiv \eta_t - \eta_0 + \alpha(1-q_t) + \beta S_{t-1},
     \label{eq:implicit_eta} \\
  0 &= g_{3,t}(\theta_r)
     \equiv S_t - \rho S_{t-1}
       - \gamma C_t^A/Y_t^N + \delta C_t^R/Y_t^N.
     \label{eq:implicit_stress}
\end{align}
Equations \eqref{eq:implicit_xc}--\eqref{eq:implicit_stress} are not a full
structural model. They are a practical identification layer. They say that the
headline variables only become useful when the reference parameters and the
latent states are tied back to data.

In plain language, this block does three jobs. Equation
\eqref{eq:implicit_xc} says that headroom is not observed directly. We infer
it from output, capacity, and entropy after we choose a regional reference
point. Equation \eqref{eq:implicit_eta} says that efficiency should fall when
asset-directed credit and stress become too strong. Equation
\eqref{eq:implicit_stress} says that fragility should rise when asset-directed
credit keeps building up, and should ease when credit moves back to
GDP-linked uses.

For the current repository implementation we therefore use a lightweight,
iterative calibration step rather than a one-time manual choice. For each
region $r$, the working objective is
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{L}_r(\theta_r)
   &= w_g \Bigl[1-\operatorname{corr}\!\bigl(X_C^{\theta_r}(t), \Delta U_{t+h}\bigr)\Bigr]
    + w_s \Bigl[1+\operatorname{corr}\!\bigl(X_C^{\theta_r}(t), \Delta \mathrm{spread}_{t+h}\bigr)\Bigr] \nonumber \\
   &\quad
    + w_\ell \Bigl[1+\operatorname{corr}\!\bigl(X_C^{\theta_r}(t), |\mathrm{loop\_area}_{t+h}|\bigr)\Bigr]
    + w_T \Bigl[1-\operatorname{corr}\!\bigl(X_C^{\theta_r}(t), T_{L,t}\bigr)\Bigr]
    + w_R \|\theta_r-\theta_r^{(0)}\|^2,
  \label{eq:calibration_objective}
\end{align}
with horizon $h=4$ quarters in the current empirical pass and
$\theta_r^{(0)}$ denoting the configuration defaults. In simple terms, a useful
$X_C$ should move with later capacity growth, move against later spread
widening and dissipation, and still stay close enough to the default scale to
remain readable. The present code implements this as a small coordinate search,
not as a claim of final identification. That is deliberate: the immediate goal
is to stop $X_C$ from becoming a decorative transform and to make the
parameter choice open to testing.

In the implementation, the level anchors $(U_0,V_0,S_0)$ are first re-centered
on each region's own sample medians before the iterative search begins. This is
important in practice. Public regional panels often mix quantities with very
different nominal scales, and the raw configuration constants can otherwise
make $X_C$ look like a unit-conversion artifact. The tuned score should
therefore be read as an \emph{implicit headroom index}: it keeps the sign logic
of $X_C$, but it is identified from local deviations around observed regional
baselines rather than from fixed absolute constants.

This also gives a simple reading rule for the results section. If the tuned
implicit headroom score moves while the raw pipeline $X_C$ stays flat or
clipped, the original fixed-parameter transform is too rigid for that region.
If the tuned score still fails to improve the relation to future growth or
stress, then the thermo-style layer is not adding enough value.

\section{Limits, Identification, and Scope}\label{sec:limitations}
This construction is intentionally modest. Key limitations include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Category dependence.} $\SM$ depends on the chosen partition
        of uses. Results should be checked across other reasonable partitions.

  \item \textbf{Proxy noise.} $\TL$, $\VC$, $\pC$ are built from noisy
        proxies, and $U$ itself is instantiated from GDP- or
        credit-like aggregates. Measurement error or proxy misspecification
        can break \eqref{eq:Maxwell} and distort the decomposition
        \eqref{eq:first_law}; in practice these relations are treated as
        reference structures and consistency checks rather than hard
        identities.

  \item \textbf{Local-equilibrium vs. non-equilibrium.} Fast crises and regime
        shifts violate smooth state-variable assumptions. We therefore interpret
        the Maxwell-like relations as local limit cases and treat
        vorticity and hysteresis measures as the main non-equilibrium outputs.

  \item \textbf{Non-physical.} We do not assume microscopic ``money
        particles'' or claim that macro-financial data obey physical first/
        second laws. The mapping is an analogy and is judged only by
        empirical usefulness.

  \item \textbf{Identification challenges.} Policy, expectations, and shocks
        act jointly. Causal claims need careful research design beyond
        the bookkeeping framework presented here.

  \item \textbf{Partition measurement.} The coarse split $(C_t^R,C_t^A)$ is
        often only partially observed in public data. In many applications,
        $q_t$ must be proxy-estimated or treated as a latent state, so reported
        results should separate direct observations from inferred quantities.
        This matters especially for construction credit, which often sits
        between GDP-linked and existing-asset uses.

  \item \textbf{Scaling conventions.} Constants such as $k$ are
        conventional. Report normalized metrics and sensitivity checks.
\end{itemize}

This version should therefore be read as a theory-and-implementation note with
explicit rejection conditions. If the partition variables
$(C_t^R,C_t^A,q_t)$ and the stress state $S_t$ fail to improve empirical
discrimination relative to simpler credit-volume baselines, the
measurement-first version of Thermo Credit should be simplified or rejected in
that application. A more systematic empirical evaluation for the JP
configuration is left to a later revision.

\paragraph{Data-source map.}

mapping is stored in \texttt{data/data\_dictionary.csv}.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Raw and feature tables.} Region-specific builder scripts
        \texttt{scripts/01\_build\_features.py} (JP),
        \texttt{scripts/04\_build\_features\_eu.py} (EU), and
        \texttt{scripts/05\_build\_features\_us.py} (US) select source
        series according to configuration files
        (\texttt{config.yml}, \texttt{config\_\{jp,eu,us\}.yml}) and
        \texttt{lib/series\_selector.py}. They write money, credit,
        regulatory-pressure, and allocation tables under \texttt{data/}
        (e.g.\ \texttt{money*.csv}, \texttt{credit*.csv},
        \texttt{reg\_pressure*.csv}, \texttt{allocation\_q*.csv}).
  \item \textbf{Entropy and temperature.} The module
        \texttt{lib/entropy.py} implements $\SM = k \Min H(q)$ from
        \eqref{eq:SM_def} and also computes
        $S_{\mathrm{M,in}}$, $S_{\mathrm{M,out}}$,
        $S_{\mathrm{M,hat}} = H(q)/\log K$, and optional per-bucket
        contributions. The module \texttt{lib/temperature.py} builds $\TL$
        from credit spreads, depth, and turnover, combines the standardised
        inputs into $\hat T = z_s z_d (1 + \tfrac{1}{2} z_t)$, and then
        applies min--max normalisation.
  \item \textbf{Internal-energy gauge.}
        In the credit tables, the column $U(t)$ acts as the internal-energy or
        capacity proxy used in \eqref{eq:first_law}, \eqref{eq:FC_def}, and
        \eqref{eq:XC_def}. When a dedicated $U$ series is missing, the code
        fills it conservatively: first from an explicit $U$ column, then from
        GDP-like series such as $U_{\text{gdp\_only}}$ or $Y$, and only as a
        last resort from $L_{\mathrm{real}}$. An optional detrended version
        $U^\star$ subtracts a no-lookahead rolling mean.
  \item \textbf{Depth and turnover enrichment.}
        When region-specific depth/turnover series are not supplied, the
        helper \texttt{lib/credit\_enrichment.py} constructs depth and
        turnover heuristically from real credit stocks $L_{\mathrm{real}}$
        and capacity proxy $U$ as described in the main text.
  \item \textbf{Measurement-first tool layer.}
        The module \texttt{lib/thermo\_credit\_tools.py} provides the compact
        v2 interface used by the repository-side AI layer: theory overview,
        variable definitions, metric computation, scenario evaluation, and
        regime comparison. The script
        \texttt{scripts/thermo\_credit\_cli.py} exposes the same layer as a
        JSON CLI for local checks.
  \item \textbf{MCP distribution wrapper.}
        The module \texttt{lib/thermo\_credit\_mcp.py} wraps the same core
        calculations as MCP resources, tools, and prompt templates, and
        \texttt{scripts/thermo\_credit\_mcp\_server.py} provides a runnable
        server entrypoint. This layer is meant for AI accessibility and
        structured reuse. It does not replace the empirical validation logic in
        the main text.
  \item \textbf{Capacity, headroom, and coupling.} The core constructor
        \texttt{lib/indicators.py} merges money, allocation, credit, and
        regulatory tables, renames regulatory quantities into
        $\VC$ and $\pC$, and, when configured with the ``min\_headroom''
        formula, sets
        $\VC(t) = \min_j H_j(t)$ over headroom components.
        External pressure/temperature drivers are aggregated in\\
        \texttt{lib/external\_coupling.py}
        into $E_p$ and $E_T$; when the
        coupling coefficients $\alpha,\delta$ are non-zero the code applies
        the affine shifts
        $p_{\mathrm{C}} \leftarrow p_{\mathrm{C}}^{\text{baseline}} +
        \alpha E_p$ and
        $T_{\mathrm{L}} \leftarrow T_{\mathrm{L}}^{\text{baseline}} +
        \delta E_T$.
        The same module computes $\FC$, $\XC$, and the Gibbs-style $\GC$
        and streams the loop-area estimator in $(\pC,\VC)$.
  \item \textbf{Diagnostics.}
        The diagnostics routine in \texttt{lib/indicators.py} 
        estimates $\partial S_{\mathrm{M}}/\partial V_{\mathrm{C}}|_{T_{\mathrm{L}}}$
        and $\partial p_{\mathrm{C}}/\partial T_{\mathrm{L}}|_{V_{\mathrm{C}}}$
        via rolling least squares, and constructs finite-difference versions
        of the first-law decomposition by computing
        $\Delta U$, $\Delta S_{\mathrm{M}}$, $\Delta V_{\mathrm{C}}$,
        $\bar T_{\mathrm{L}}$, $\bar p_{\mathrm{C}}$, and the implied
        $Q_{\text{like}}$ and $W_{\text{like}}$ series.
  \item \textbf{Per-region orchestration.}
        Scripts \texttt{scripts/02\_compute\_indicators.py},\\
        \texttt{scripts/02\_compute\_indicators\_eu.py},\\
        \texttt{scripts/02\_compute\_indicators\_us.py}, and the helper
        \texttt{lib/regions.py} assemble the inputs for JP/EU/US and write
        \texttt{site/indicators*.csv} used by the dashboard and tests.
  \item \textbf{Theory calibration layer.}
        The module \texttt{lib/theory\_calibration.py} and script
        \texttt{scripts/07\_calibrate\_theory\_model.py} perform a lightweight
        iterative search over $(T_0,p_0,U_0,V_0,S_0)$, write
        \texttt{data/calibrated\_theory\_params.json}, and emit the
        LaTeX snippets consumed in the empirical snapshot and calibration
        sections of the note.
  \item \textbf{Fallbacks and CI reproducibility.} To keep tests and scheduled
        builds deterministic, \texttt{scripts/ci\_prepare\_minimal\_data.py}
        seeds tiny synthetic CSVs when real inputs are absent, and the
        GitHub Actions workflows re-run the pipeline under pinned
        dependencies so that indicators and the report can be regenerated
        from a fixed environment.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Appendix B: Glossary (For Physicists New to Finance)}

\paragraph{Numbering.}
Each term is assigned a unique K-number (K1, K2, \ldots) in the order it is
first referenced in the main text. Use the superscript K\# markers to jump
back here; headings show the same inline K\#.

This glossary explains the main banking, credit, and regulatory terms used in
this note for readers with a physics background. The definitions are informal
and written for intuition. Where useful, we add analogies to thermodynamics or
dynamical systems. These analogies are only guides, not literal identities.

\subsection*{1.\ Regulation and Bank Balance Sheets}

\paragraph{K1 CET1 (Common Equity Tier 1).}
This is the core equity capital of a bank, such as common shares and retained
earnings. It absorbs losses and supports solvency. Analogy: the bank's basic
``energy reserve''.

\paragraph{K2 RWA (Risk-Weighted Assets).}
These are total assets weighted by regulatory risk factors. Riskier exposures
get higher weights. The ratio CET1/RWA is one of the main prudential metrics.
Analogy: an effective load or mass adjusted for fragility.

\paragraph{K3 CET1/RWA ratio and slack.}
The CET1/RWA ratio shows how much high-quality capital backs risk-weighted
assets. Regulation sets a minimum. The slack is the amount above that minimum,
and it measures capacity to expand or absorb shocks. Analogy: a safety margin
or unused headroom in a constrained system.

\paragraph{K4 Credit capacity / Headroom ($\VC$).}
This is the effective room for additional lending, given capital, liquidity,
and internal limits. In this note, $\VC$ is the state-like variable that
captures that remaining room. Analogy: usable volume before the system hits a
hard wall.

\paragraph{K5 Credit pressure ($\pC$).}
This is the shadow price of capacity: the marginal cost or value of relaxing
or tightening $\VC$. It is defined by analogy to
$\pC = -(\partial U / \partial \VC)_{\SM}$ or $-\partial F / \partial \VC$.
Analogy: thermodynamic pressure; tighter constraints imply higher $\pC$.

\paragraph{K6 HQLA (High Quality Liquid Assets).}
These are very liquid, low-risk assets, such as government bonds, that can be
sold quickly in stress. They support liquidity ratios and crisis resilience.
Analogy: high-grade stored energy that can be tapped on demand.

\paragraph{K7 Liquidity buffer.}
This is cash plus HQLA held as an emergency reserve against sudden outflows or
market stress. Analogy: a backup battery or safety reservoir.

\paragraph{K8 LCR (Liquidity Coverage Ratio).}
This regulatory ratio is HQLA divided by projected net cash outflows over a
30-day stress scenario. It tests short-term liquidity robustness. Analogy: can
the system keep running for 30 days under worst-case load?

\paragraph{K9 NSFR (Net Stable Funding Ratio).}
This regulatory ratio looks over a one-year horizon. It compares stable
funding with required stable funding and limits excessive maturity mismatch.
Analogy: making sure long-lived assets are backed by sufficiently slow-decaying
sources.

\subsection*{2.\ Money, Credit, and Flow Concepts}

\paragraph{K10 Money-in-circulation.}
This is an operational measure of money that is actually circulating in the
chosen system and horizon, for example deposits used in payments. It is not
just base money on the central bank balance sheet. Analogy: active particles
taking part in interactions.

\paragraph{K11 Credit stocks and flows.}
Stocks are the outstanding amounts of loans or credit at a given time. Flows
are the changes per period, such as new lending, repayments, or net issuance.
Analogy: internal energy versus power or energy flux.

\paragraph{K12 Margin credit / Securities financing.}
This is credit used to finance positions in securities, such as margin loans
or some repo activity. It amplifies leverage in financial markets and is often
separated from credit to the real economy.

\paragraph{K13 Commercial Paper (CP).}
This is short-term unsecured debt issued by firms. It provides funding outside
traditional bank loans. Analogy: an alternative branch in the credit circuit.

\paragraph{K14 Policy work ($W_{\mathrm{policy}}$).}
This covers deliberate interventions by central banks, regulators, or
governments: rate changes, asset purchases, guarantees, capital-rule changes,
and similar actions. In our decomposition, this is the structured and
intentional part of ``work''. Analogy: externally applied work on the system.

\subsection*{3.\ Structural and Analytical Concepts}

\paragraph{K15 MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive).}
This is a rule for defining categories: no overlaps and no gaps. For the
entropy $\SM = k \Min H(q)$, the allocation shares $q_i$ should be MECE.
Otherwise $\SM$ is distorted by double counting or omissions.

\paragraph{K16 State variable and proxy.}
A state variable is determined by the current state, not by the full path that
led there. In practice, finance uses observable proxies such as ratios and
indices to approximate such variables. Our Maxwell-like relation
\eqref{eq:Maxwell} tests whether the chosen proxies behave as if they came
from a consistent potential $U(\SM,\VC,\ldots)$.

\paragraph{K17 Hysteresis and loop area.}
If trajectories in the $(\pC,\VC)$ plane form loops with non-zero area over
policy or credit cycles, this suggests irreversibility or dissipation-like
effects, such as stress, misallocation, or path dependence. Analogy: magnetic
hysteresis, frictional cycles, or inelastic processes.

\paragraph{K18 Stress testing and early-warning indicators.}
Stress tests simulate severe but plausible scenarios to see whether banks or
systems can survive them. Early-warning indicators try to flag fragility in
advance. Within this framework, quantities like $\FC$, $\XC$, and loop areas
are meant as structured candidates to complement such tools.

\paragraph{K19 Money vs.\ credit (QTM vs.\ QTC perspective).}
In traditional Quantity Theory of Money (QTM), money is primary and credit is
often treated as derivative. In the Quantity Theory of Credit (QTC), and in
this note, credit creation is primary: bank lending creates deposits, and
money is an accounting outcome of credit decisions. This shift motivates
tracking credit capacity and dispersion as state-like objects.
